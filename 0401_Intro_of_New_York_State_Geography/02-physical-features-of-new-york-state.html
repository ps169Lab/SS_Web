Physical Features of New York State

Exploring New York's Major Biomes and Landforms

Vocabulary Words

  • Landform - a natural feature of Earth's surface like mountains or valleys
  • Biome - a large area with similar climate, plants, and animals
  • Glacier - a huge sheet of ice that moves slowly over land
  • Estuary - where a river meets the ocean and mixes fresh and salt water

How New York's Landforms Were Made

Landforms are natural features of Earth's surface, including mountains, valleys, rivers, and lakes. New York State exhibits remarkable topographical diversity, shaped by millions of years of geological processes. The state's varied landscape results from glacial activity, tectonic forces, and erosion over extensive time periods.


A long, long time ago, giant sheets of ice called glaciers moved back and forth across New York many times. When these huge ice sheets melted about 10,000 years ago, they left behind special land shapes like the Hudson River valley, the Finger Lakes, and piles of rocks and dirt that we can still see today!

Mountain Regions

New York contains two major mountain systems that significantly influence regional climate and ecology. These elevated regions create diverse habitats and affect precipitation patterns across the state.


The Adirondack Mountains occupy northeastern New York and contain Mount Marcy, the state's highest peak at 5,344 feet. The Adirondack Park encompasses six million acres, making it larger than Yellowstone, Everglades, and Grand Canyon National Parks combined. The Catskill Mountains feature older, more rounded peaks with numerous waterfalls and fast-flowing streams.


Water Systems

New York's extensive network of rivers and lakes provides crucial water resources and transportation routes. These water bodies were largely shaped by glacial activity and continue to influence human settlement patterns and economic development.


The Hudson River flows 315 miles from the Adirondacks to New York Harbor, serving as a major transportation corridor. The Finger Lakes region contains eleven glacially-carved lakes that extend north-south through central New York. These deep lakes moderate local climate and support agriculture, particularly viticulture.


New York touches two Great Lakes - Erie and Ontario - which hold about 20% of all the fresh water on Earth's surface! The Niagara River connects these lakes and creates the famous Niagara Falls, where water drops 167 feet down over rocky cliffs - that's like falling off a 16-story building!

Lowland Regions

New York's lowland areas include coastal plains and lake plains that provide fertile agricultural land and sites for major population centers. These relatively flat regions contrast sharply with the state's mountainous areas.


Long Island is made of stuff left behind by glaciers that forms a flat area near the coast. The flat lands near Buffalo used to be the bottom of ancient lakes! These areas are great for farming and building cities because the land is flat and the soil is really good for growing things.

Environmental Impact

New York's many different land shapes create different weather patterns and homes for plants and animals throughout the state. Mountain areas are cooler and get more rain and snow, while flat areas are perfect for farming and building cities. All these different landscapes help support many different kinds of plants, animals, and ways for people to make a living!
