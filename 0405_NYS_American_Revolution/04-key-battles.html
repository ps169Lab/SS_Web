New York at War

From the Streets of Brooklyn to the Forests of Saratoga

Expert Vocabulary

  • Campaign: A series of military battles or operations moved toward a single goal.
  • Intelligence: Secret information collected about an enemy.
  • Turning Point: An event that causes a significant change in the course of a war.

The Battle for the City (1776)

In the summer of 1776, the largest British fleet ever seen arrived in New York Harbor. The British goal was to capture New York City and use it as their headquarters. At the Battle of Brooklyn (also called the Battle of Long Island), George Washington’s army was badly defeated and forced to retreat across the East River under the cover of night. By September, the British had occupied Manhattan, a control they would keep for the next seven years. In 1776, a huge British navy arrived in New York City. They fought George Washington at the Battle of Brooklyn. Washington lost the battle and had to lead his soldiers away in the middle of the night. The British took over New York City and stayed there until the war ended.

The Turning Point at Saratoga (1777)

While the British held the city, they wanted to control the entire Hudson River to split the colonies in two. However, in October 1777, American forces won a massive victory at the Battle of Saratoga in upstate New York. This was the turning point of the war because it convinced France that the Americans could actually win. France joined the war as an ally, sending ships and soldiers to help. The British wanted to control the Hudson River. But in 1777, the Americans won a big battle at Saratoga. This was the most important win of the war. After this battle, the country of France decided to help the Americans fight.

The Secret War: The Culper Spy Ring

Because the British occupied New York City, Washington needed intelligence on what they were planning. He helped create the Culper Spy Ring, a group of regular people on Long Island and in the city who gathered secrets. They used "invisible ink" and secret codes to communicate. One famous member, Anna Strong, reportedly used her laundry line as a signal. By hanging a black petticoat or specific numbers of handkerchiefs, she told other spies where to meet or if a message was ready to be picked up. George Washington needed to know the British secrets. He used a group of spies called the Culper Spy Ring. These spies used secret codes and invisible ink. A woman named Anna Strong used her laundry to send signals! She would hang up a black petticoat to tell other spies that it was safe to talk.
