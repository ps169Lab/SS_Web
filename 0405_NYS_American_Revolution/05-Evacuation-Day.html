The British Leave at Last

Evacuation Day: November 25, 1783

Expert Vocabulary

  • Evacuation: The act of moving people or troops away from a place.
  • Occupation: When a foreign army takes control of a city or country and stays there.
  • Symbol: An object or action that stands for an idea, like a flag standing for freedom.

The Long Wait is Over

After seven long years of British occupation, the Revolutionary War finally came to an end. On November 25, 1783, the last British soldiers boarded their ships in New York Harbor to sail back to England. This event is known as Evacuation Day. For the people who had lived through the war, seeing the British fleet depart was a moment of pure joy and relief. The city they loved was finally back in American hands. The war was finally over! The British soldiers had lived in New York City for seven years. On November 25, 1783, they got on their ships and left. This was called Evacuation Day. The people in the city were very happy to be free.

The Mystery of the Greased Flagpole

As the British were leaving, they tried to play one last trick on the Americans. They nailed a British flag to the top of a flagpole at Fort George (near Battery Park) and greased the pole with oil so no one could climb it. They wanted the Americans to watch the British flag fly as the ships sailed away. However, a brave American soldier named John Van Arsdale used iron cleats to climb the slippery pole. He ripped down the British flag and raised the American stars and stripes while the crowd cheered! The British tried to play a trick before they left. They put their flag at the top of a very slippery, oily pole. They thought no one could climb it. But a brave soldier used special shoes to climb up. He took down the British flag and put up the American flag!

A Hero's Welcome

Later that day, General George Washington led a grand parade from Harlem all the way down to the Battery. The streets were filled with New Yorkers who had stayed through the hard years of the war. That evening, Washington joined other leaders for a victory dinner at Fraunces Tavern in lower Manhattan. For the next 100 years, Evacuation Day was celebrated in New York City with more parades and fireworks than any other holiday! George Washington led a big parade through the streets of New York. Everyone came out to cheer for him. That night, they had a big dinner at Fraunces Tavern to celebrate. For a long time, this was the most famous holiday in New York City.
