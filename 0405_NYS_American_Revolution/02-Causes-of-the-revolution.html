The Road to Revolution

Taxation Without Representation: New York Finds its Voice

Expert Vocabulary

  • Protest: An action that shows you strongly disagree with something.
  • Boycott: Refusing to buy or use something as a way to protest.
  • Repeal: To officially cancel or take back a law.

The Stamp Act Congress in NYC

In 1765, the British Parliament passed the Stamp Act. This law required colonists to pay for a special British stamp on every piece of printed paper they used, including legal documents, newspapers, and even playing cards[cite: 365]. New York City became the center of the resistance when delegates from nine colonies met at City Hall (now Federal Hall) in Manhattan. This meeting, called the Stamp Act Congress, was the first time the colonies worked together to send a unified message to the King[cite: 363, 881]. In 1765, the King made a new rule called the Stamp Act. Colonists had to pay for a special stamp on all paper, like newspapers and cards. Leaders from different colonies met in New York City to talk about it. They decided to work together to tell the King the law was not fair.

Protests and Tea Parties

Groups like the Sons of Liberty were very active in New York City, organizing boycotts and challenging British authority. While the most famous "Tea Party" happened in Boston in 1773 to protest tea taxes, New York had its own protests. Colonists refused to let British ships unload their tea in New York Harbor. They were determined to prove that the King could not tax them without their consent. Groups like the Sons of Liberty started protests in New York City. They told people not to buy British goods. When the King tried to tax tea, colonists in Boston and New York got angry. In Boston, they threw tea into the ocean! New Yorkers also refused to let British tea ships into the city.

The Intolerable Acts

The King was furious about the tea protests. To punish the colonies, Parliament passed a series of harsh laws known as the Intolerable Acts. These laws closed Boston Harbor and took away the colonists' right to govern themselves. Instead of scaring the people of New York, these laws made them even more united with their neighbors in Massachusetts. The "mental box" for many New Yorkers was shifting—they no longer felt like British subjects; they were becoming Americans. The King was very angry about the tea protests. He made new, mean laws called the Intolerable Acts. He closed the harbor in Boston and took away people's rights. The people in New York felt sorry for Boston. These mean laws made all the colonies want to stand together against the King.
