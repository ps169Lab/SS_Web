Rights Denied to Women

The Legal Barriers of the 1800s

Key Schema Terms

  • Coverture: A law where a husband legally "covered" his wife, meaning she had no rights of her own.
  • Suffrage: The right to vote.
  • Civilly Dead: How the law treated married women—as if they didn't exist in court.

The "Invisible" Person

In the 1800s, New York laws followed a rule called Coverture. When a woman married, she and her husband became "one person" in the eyes of the law—and that person was the husband. A married woman was considered "civilly dead." She could not own property, sign a contract, or even keep the money she earned at a job. Long ago, a law called "coverture" took away a woman's rights when she got married. The law said the husband owned everything. A married woman could not own a house or keep the money she earned.

Locked Doors: Education and Voting

Equality was also blocked in public life. Women were denied Suffrage, meaning they had no say in the laws that governed them. Most colleges were for men only, which prevented women from becoming doctors or lawyers. If a woman did find work, she was often paid half of what a man made for the same task. Women were not allowed to vote. Most big schools were for men only, so women could not be doctors or lawyers. Even when women worked, they were paid much less than men.

Cracks in the Wall

Change began slowly in New York. In 1848, the Married Women's Property Act allowed women to finally keep property they owned before marriage. While it didn't give women the vote, it was the first time the law recognized a married woman as an individual. This small victory gave a spark to the movement for full equality. New York began to change in 1848. A new law said women could finally keep their own property. It was a small step, but it gave women hope for more freedom.
