Life as an Enslaved Person in New York

The Long Road Toward Freedom and Equality

Expert Vocabulary

  • Enslaved: Being forced to work for no pay and having no rights to your own life or choices.
  • Resistance: Finding ways to fight back against unfair treatment, either through small actions or large protests.
  • Emancipation: The official act of being set free from slavery.
  • Urban Slavery: In New York, many enslaved people worked in busy cities rather than just on large farms.

The Arrival: Slavery in the North

Slavery is often associated with the South, but it was a major part of New York’s history for over 200 years. The Dutch brought the first enslaved Africans to New Amsterdam in 1626. By 1703, New York City had the second-largest enslaved population in the American colonies. Enslaved people were the ones who cleared the forests to create Broadway and built the wall that gives Wall Street its name. Many people think slavery was only in the South, but it was in New York too. The first enslaved people were brought here in 1626. They did the hard work of building the city, including famous places like Broadway and Wall Street.

Daily Life and Hardship

Enslaved people in New York worked in almost every job imaginable. In the city, they were blacksmiths, carpenters, and sailors. In the countryside, they worked on large farms in the Hudson Valley. Unlike on large southern plantations, enslaved people in New York often lived in the same houses as their owners, sleeping in cold attics or damp cellars with very little privacy. Enslaved people in New York had many jobs. They were builders, farmers, and workers on ships. Most lived in small, cold spaces like attics or basements in their owners' houses. They worked very hard every day without pay.

History Under Our Feet: The African Burial Ground

In 1991, workers in Lower Manhattan discovered the remains of over 400 enslaved people buried beneath the city streets. Today, this is the African Burial Ground National Monument. It serves as a reminder of the thousands of people who helped build New York but were denied their freedom.

Resistance and the Fight for Freedom

Enslaved New Yorkers never stopped resisting. Some used active resistance, like the Revolt of 1712, where they fought back against their owners. Others used passive resistance, such as working slowly, breaking tools, or learning to read and write in secret. Every act of hope was a way of saying that they deserved to be free. Enslaved people fought back in many ways. Some tried to escape to find a better life. Others found small ways to resist, like breaking tools or helping each other in secret. They never gave up hope.

The Path to Emancipation

After the Revolution, more people in New York began to realize that slavery was wrong. However, the state did not end it all at once. They used a "gradual" process. A law in 1799 said that children born to enslaved mothers would eventually be free, but they still had to work for their owners until they were young adults. Finally, on July 4, 1827, slavery was completely abolished in New York State. New York slowly began to end slavery after the Revolutionary War. A law was passed to help children become free. Finally, in 1827, slavery officially ended in New York. People celebrated their freedom, but the fight for equal rights was just beginning.
