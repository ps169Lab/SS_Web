The Drafting of the Constitution

Philadelphia 1787: Designing a New Government

Expert Vocabulary

  • Framers: The group of leaders who designed and wrote the U.S. Constitution.
  • Compromise: An agreement where both sides give up a little of what they want to solve a problem.
  • Ratify: To give formal approval to a law or document so it can take effect.
  • Federalist: Someone who wanted a strong national government (like New York's Alexander Hamilton).

The Meeting in Philadelphia

In the sweltering summer of 1787, 55 delegates gathered at the Constitutional Convention in Philadelphia. Their goal was to fix the Articles of Confederation, which had left the national government too weak to collect taxes or settle fights between states. While they met in Independence Hall—the same place where the Declaration of Independence was signed—their mission was different: they were building a "mental box" for how a nation should run. In 1787, leaders met in Philadelphia for the Constitutional Convention. The old rules for the country were not working. These leaders wanted to write a new plan to help the states work together and stay safe.

New York's Voice: Alexander Hamilton

New York sent Alexander Hamilton as one of its delegates. As a Federalist, Hamilton argued that America needed a powerful central government to handle money and defense. He worried that if the states stayed too independent, the country would fall apart. He worked closely with other "Framers" like James Madison and Benjamin Franklin to design the three branches of government. Alexander Hamilton was a famous leader from New York. He wanted the national government to be strong. He worked with other leaders like James Madison and Benjamin Franklin to write the new rules.

The Great Compromise

The biggest argument was about how many votes each state should get. Large states like New York wanted more votes because they had more people. Small states wanted every state to be equal. They finally reached the Great Compromise. They created the House of Representatives (based on population) and the Senate (where every state gets two votes). The states argued about votes. Big states wanted more votes. Small states wanted things to be equal. They made a compromise. They made two groups for making laws so that everyone felt the system was fair.

The Three-Fifths Compromise

A deep disagreement occurred over slavery. Southern states wanted to count enslaved people to get more votes in Congress, while Northern states disagreed. This led to the Three-Fifths Compromise. While this kept the convention moving, it created a massive problem for the nation's future. The Framers also included checks and balances to make sure no one branch became as powerful as a King.
