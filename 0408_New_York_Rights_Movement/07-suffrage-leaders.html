Suffrage Leaders

The Brave Women Who Fought for the Right to Vote

Movement Vocabulary

  • Suffrage: The legal right to vote in elections.
  • Petition: A formal written request, signed by many people, to a government.
  • Amendment: An official change or addition to the Constitution.

The Great Partnership

The fight for women's suffrage was led by a "dynamic duo" in New York: Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Susan B. Anthony. They met in 1851 and became lifelong friends. Elizabeth was the "writer"—she stayed home with her seven children and wrote powerful speeches. Susan was the "traveler"—she spent 50 years traveling across the country giving those speeches. Together, they proved that women could organize powerful political movements even without the vote. Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Susan B. Anthony were a great team. Elizabeth wrote the speeches, and Susan traveled the country to give them. They worked for over 50 years to win the right to vote.

Diverse Leaders, One Goal

While Stanton and Anthony were the most famous, many other New York women provided the "words" and "action" needed to change the laws. They used newspapers, fashion, and even public speaking to challenge the idea that women were not equal to men. Many different women helped the movement. Some used newspapers and some used speeches. They all wanted to show that women were equal to men.

Leader Their "Mental Box" Contribution
Lucretia Mott The Wise Mentor A Quaker minister who was a famous public speaker long before the movement started.
Amelia Bloomer The Reformer Published the newspaper The Lily and promoted "bloomers" (pants) so women could move more freely.
Matilda Joslyn Gage The Historian Researched and wrote the history of the movement so future generations wouldn't forget their work.

Connecting the Fights

These leaders didn't just fight for themselves. Many were Abolitionists first. For example, during the Civil War, Susan B. Anthony organized a petition with 400,000 signatures to end slavery. These women believed that equality was a single "mental box"—if one group was denied freedom, everyone's freedom was at risk. They faced decades of ridicule, but their partnership proved that organized "agitation" could eventually change the world. These women also worked to end slavery. They believed that everyone deserved to be free and equal. Even when people were mean to them, they never gave up.
