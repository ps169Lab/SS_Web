The Dutch West India Company

The Business That Built New Netherlands

Vocabulary Words

  • Company - a business organization
  • Settlement - a place where people live permanently
  • Colony - land controlled by another country
  • Profit - money made from business
  • Charter - official permission from the government

What Was the Dutch West India Company?

The Dutch West India Company was a powerful business organization that controlled trade and colonization in the Americas. Founded in 1621, this company had permission from the Dutch government to build settlements, trade with Native Americans, and make profits in the New World. It was like a giant business that also acted like a government.

The company's main goal was to make money through trade, especially the valuable fur trade. They also wanted to compete with other European countries like Spain and Portugal who were already making fortunes in America. The Dutch government gave the company special rights to control all Dutch trade in the Americas.

The First Dutch Settlement: Fort Nassau

In 1614, the Dutch built their first permanent settlement in New York at Fort Nassau, near present-day Albany. This fort was built by Hendrick Christiaensen under the company's orders. Fort Nassau was strategically placed where the Mohawk and Hudson Rivers meet, making it perfect for controlling the fur trade with Native American tribes.

Fort Nassau was small but important. It had thick wooden walls for protection and buildings to store valuable beaver furs. Dutch traders lived there year-round, making it the first European settlement in what would become New York State. Later, the Dutch built the larger Fort Orange nearby to replace Fort Nassau.

Building New Amsterdam

The Dutch West India Company later built Fort Amsterdam in 1625 on Manhattan Island. This fort grew into the town of New Amsterdam, which we now call New York City. The company brought families from the Netherlands to live there and work for the business.

The company chose Manhattan because it controlled both the Hudson River valley and the harbor that would prove crucial for trade and defense. Ships could easily sail up the Hudson River to Fort Orange or out to sea to trade with Europe.

How the Company Worked

The Dutch West India Company was run like a business, not like other colonies. Company officials made the rules, collected taxes, and controlled all trade. They hired governors to run the colony and soldiers to protect it. Everything was designed to make the company rich through the fur trade with Native Americans.

The company also used the patroon system, which granted large land holdings to wealthy investors who agreed to bring settlers to work the land. This system helped populate the colony but also created large estates along the Hudson River that would last for many years.

The End of Company Rule

In 1664, the British conquered New Netherland and ended Dutch West India Company rule. The company had been successful in establishing the fur trade and founding settlements, but it never made as much money as hoped. However, the foundations they laid - the cities, trade relationships, and diverse population - would shape New York's development for centuries to come.
