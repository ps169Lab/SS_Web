Slavery in the New Colony

A Sad and Wrong Part of Early American History

Vocabulary Words

  • Slavery - forcing people to work without pay
  • Enslaved person - someone forced to work as property
  • Freedom - the right to make your own choices
  • Labor - hard work, especially physical work
  • Injustice - something that is unfair and wrong

Slavery Comes to New Netherlands

Slavery was a terrible and unfair system where people were forced to work without pay and were treated as property. In the Dutch colony of New Netherlands, enslaved people were brought from Africa to work on farms, build houses, and help with trade. This was completely wrong, but it happened because colonists wanted cheap labor to make their businesses successful.

The first enslaved Africans arrived in New Amsterdam in 1626, just one year after the Dutch built Fort Amsterdam. The Dutch West India Company brought them to work on company projects like building forts and clearing land. By 1664, about 20% of New Amsterdam's population were enslaved people.

What Work Did Enslaved People Do?

Enslaved people in New Netherlands did many different jobs. They worked on farms growing crops like wheat and corn, built houses and roads, loaded and unloaded ships at the harbor, and worked as servants in wealthy people's homes. Some learned special skills like carpentry or cooking, but they were never paid for their hard work.

Enslaved people also helped with the fur trade, carrying heavy bundles of furs and helping to transport goods between trading posts and ships. Their labor was essential to making the Dutch colony successful, even though they received no benefits from their hard work.

Life for Enslaved People

Life was very hard for enslaved people. They lived in poor conditions, had little food, and could be bought and sold like objects. Families were often separated when family members were sold to different owners. Despite these terrible conditions, enslaved people tried to maintain their cultures, religions, and family connections.

Under Dutch rule, enslaved families were usually kept together, and they could join churches and get married. But when the English took control in 1664, life became much harder. New laws said enslaved people could not travel, trade, or buy things without permission from their owners.

Fighting for Freedom

Even though life was very difficult, enslaved people never stopped hoping for freedom. Some tried to escape to find better lives. In 1712, a group of enslaved New Yorkers fought back against their treatment in what was called the New York Slave Revolt. Though it was not successful, it showed that enslaved people would never stop wanting their freedom.

The Path to Freedom

After the American Revolution, New York slowly began to end slavery. In 1799, the state passed a law saying that children born to enslaved mothers would eventually be free, but they had to work for many years first. Finally, in 1827, slavery officially ended in New York State.

It's important to learn about slavery because it was a major injustice in American history. Understanding this helps us appreciate freedom and equality today. Many enslaved people showed incredible courage and strength, and their contributions helped build America, even though they were treated unfairly.
