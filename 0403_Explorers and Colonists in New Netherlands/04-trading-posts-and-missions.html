Beavers, Forts, and New Beginnings

How the Fur Trade Built New York

Expert Vocabulary

  • Trading Post: A place where people meet to swap goods, like tools for animal furs.
  • Mission: A religious settlement where people lived to spread their faith.
  • Barter: To trade one item for another without using money.

The "Brown Gold" of New York

Long before New York had skyscrapers, it had something even more valuable to Europeans: the North American Beaver. In the 1600s, beaver fur was the height of fashion in Europe because it was used to make expensive, waterproof felt hats. Trading Posts and Forts

To make money from this "brown gold," the Dutch and French built trading posts. These were small stores near rivers where European traders met Native Americans to barter items like metal pots and tools for beaver pelts. Because these furs were worth a lot of money, settlers built forts with high wooden walls, like Fort Orange, to keep their goods safe. Settlers built trading posts near rivers. These were like small stores. They traded tools for beaver skins. The furs were worth a lot of money. Settlers built forts with big wooden walls to keep the furs safe.

Missions and New Homes

While the Dutch were focused on the business of the fur trade, French explorers in Northern New York also built missions. These were religious settlements where priests lived and worked among the Native people. Whether they came for money or for faith, the "fur fever" for beavers is the main reason the first permanent settlements were built in our state. Some people came to New York for business. Others came for their religion. They built missions to live and pray. The beaver trade is the main reason people first started building homes in New York.
