Peru as a Spanish Colony

The Age of the Viceroyalty (1532–1824)

Expert Vocabulary

Viceroyalty
A colony ruled by a "Viceroy," who was a leader chosen to act in place of the King.
Conquistador
A Spanish word for "conqueror"—the soldiers who took over the land.
Colonial
The time period when one country (like Spain) rules over another land (like Peru).
Rebellion
When people fight back against the leaders in charge to try and change the rules.

A Sudden Change

The Colonial period began when Spanish soldiers arrived in Peru in 1532. For hundreds of years before this, the Inca Emperors ruled the land from their stone cities. But when the Spanish arrived, everything changed. Peru became a Viceroyalty of Spain, meaning it was now ruled by a king who lived thousands of miles across the ocean. About 500 years ago, soldiers from Spain came to Peru. This was the start of the Colonial time. Peru was now ruled by the King of Spain instead of the Inca rulers. This lasted for a very long time!

The Spanish Conquest

A Spanish conquistador named Francisco Pizarro led a small group of soldiers into Peru. Even though the Inca army was much larger, the Spanish had horses and metal weapons. Because the Inca Empire was already weak from a big internal fight between two royal brothers, the Spanish were able to capture the leader and take control of the empire's mountain roads and territories. A man named Pizarro led the Spanish soldiers. They had horses and strong armor. The Inca were already fighting among themselves, so it was easier for the Spanish to take over the empire.

Moving the Center of Power

To show they were in charge, the Spanish changed how Peru was run. The Inca loved the high, cold Andes mountains and kept their capital in Cusco. But the Spanish wanted to be near the warm ocean so they could sail ships back to Spain. They built a brand-new coastal capital city called Lima. The Spanish King sent a powerful leader called a Viceroy to live in Lima and run the new government. The Inca loved the high, cold mountains. But the Spanish wanted to be close to the ocean. They built a new capital city by the coast called Lima. The Viceroy lived there and made rules for the King.

Economy: Mining the Mountains

The Inca treated the Earth with great respect, farming the mountain slopes on terraces. The Spanish, however, were focused on a different resource: treasure. They discovered that the mountain region was full of rich silver and gold. They forced the Native people to leave their farms and work in deep, dark mines. This dangerous work made Spain incredibly wealthy, and it made Lima a busy city full of riches. The Spanish wanted gold and silver. They made the local people dig deep inside the mountains for treasure. It was like playing Minecraft in real life, but it was very hard, unsafe work. It made the king very rich.

Mixing Cultures and Fighting Back

Because Spanish and Native families lived together for nearly 300 years, their cultures began to blur. Native artists blended their old Inca styles with Spanish ideas to create beautiful new art, music, and food. However, many people remained unhappy with the harsh colonial rules. Brave leaders like Túpac Amaru II led famous rebellions from the mountains to try and protect their people and win back their ancient freedom. Spanish and Native ways mixed together to make new music and art. But many people hated the harsh rules. Brave leaders led big battles to try to win back their freedom.
