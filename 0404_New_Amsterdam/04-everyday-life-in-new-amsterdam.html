Everyday Life in New Amsterdam

What Was It Like to Live in Early New York City?

Expert Vocabulary

  • Artisan: A skilled worker who makes things by hand, like a blacksmith or a baker.
  • Diverse: Having many different types of people, cultures, and languages in one place.
  • Apprentice: A young person learning a trade by working with a master.
  • Commerce: The activity of buying and selling goods, especially on a large scale.

A Busy, Diverse Town

Imagine standing at the very tip of Manhattan in 1660. New Amsterdam was a bustling town of about 1,500 people. It was incredibly diverse for its time—you could hear 18 different languages being spoken on the streets! Unlike other colonies that were mostly one religion, New Amsterdam was home to Dutch, English, French, and Jewish people, as well as enslaved and free Africans. This mix of cultures meant you could smell different foods like Dutch pancakes and German sausages cooking in the same neighborhood.


Work and The Apprentice System

There were no machines to make life easy, so everyone had to work hard. Men often worked as merchants or artisans. A boy might become an apprentice, moving into a master craftsman's house for years to learn how to build furniture or bake bread. Girls learned from their mothers how to manage a home, preserve food for the winter, and sew clothes. Even the harbor was a place of work, where sailors unloaded ships filled with goods from all over the world. Everyone had to work hard because there were no machines. Men were builders or traders. Boys became apprentices to learn how to make things like tools or bread. Girls learned how to cook and sew. Even the children had big chores to do every day.

Life Inside a Dutch Home

Dutch houses were made of brick and wood, often with tall, narrow shapes. Inside, families usually lived in just one or two rooms. The fireplace was the heart of the home—it was used for cooking, warmth, and light. Without running water, children had to carry heavy buckets from town wells every day. In the winter, the Dutch loved to have fun by ice skating on frozen ponds, a tradition they brought over from the Netherlands.

Class Discussion Questions

  • The Language Challenge: With 18 languages in town, how do you think an apprentice learned a job if his teacher spoke a different language?
  • Hard Work: Which chore from New Amsterdam sounds the hardest to you? How is it different from your chores today?
  • Culture: Why do you think ice skating was such a popular activity for the Dutch settlers? What does that tell us about their old home in Europe?
