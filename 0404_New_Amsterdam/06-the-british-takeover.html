The British Takeover

How New Amsterdam Became New York Without a Fight

Expert Vocabulary

  • Surrender: To give up or stop fighting a battle.
  • Fleet: A large group of warships sailing together.
  • Ultimatum: A final demand that says "do this, or else!"
  • Conquest: Taking control of a land or people by force.

The "Sandwich" Problem

By 1664, the British were frustrated. They had successful colonies to the North (New England) and to the South (Virginia), but the Dutch colony of New Netherland was right in the middle! It was like a "sandwich" where the Dutch were the filling. King Charles II of England wanted to connect his colonies so they could trade and travel more easily. He also wanted the profit from the fur trade, so he gave the land to his brother, the Duke of York, and told him to go take it. The British were unhappy because the Dutch lived right between their other colonies. It made it hard for the British to travel and trade. The King of England wanted the land for himself. He told his brother, the Duke of York, to go and take the colony away from the Dutch.

The Fleet Arrives

In August 1664, a fleet of four British warships carrying 300 soldiers sailed into New York Harbor. They sent an ultimatum to Peter Stuyvesant: surrender the city or be attacked. Stuyvesant was furious! He wanted to fight to keep the colony. However, the Dutch fort was old and crumbling, and he only had 150 soldiers. Even worse, the Dutch colonists refused to help him. They didn't want their homes and businesses destroyed by cannonballs. One day, four big British warships sailed into the harbor. They told the Dutch leader, Peter Stuyvesant, to give up. Stuyvesant wanted to fight, but his fort was weak and he didn't have many soldiers. The people in the town did not want to fight a war. They wanted to keep their homes safe.

Summary: Why the Dutch Surrendered

Reason for Surrender The Problem The "Mental Box" (Why it mattered)
Military Weakness The fort was falling apart and cannons were broken. They couldn't actually win a physical battle.
Business First The British promised that merchants could keep trading. Living was more important than who the "boss" was.
Stuyvesant's Leadership Stuyvesant was very strict and unpopular. The people weren't willing to die for a leader they didn't like.

New Amsterdam Becomes New York

Because the British offered "generous terms"—meaning the Dutch could keep their property, their religion, and their local laws—the colonists convinced Stuyvesant to sign the surrender papers. On September 8, 1664, the Dutch flag was lowered and the British flag was raised. New Amsterdam was immediately renamed New York in honor of the Duke of York. This was a "peaceful conquest" because not a single shot was fired! The British told the Dutch they could keep their houses and their churches if they gave up. So, the Dutch people agreed to stop fighting. They took down their flag and put up a British flag. They changed the name of the city to New York. No one was hurt, and the city changed forever.

Class Discussion Questions

  • A Difficult Choice: If you were a Dutch baker, would you fight for Peter Stuyvesant, or would you agree to let the British take over so you could keep your bakery?
  • Geography: Looking at a map of the 13 Colonies, why was New York the "missing piece" of the puzzle for the British?
  • A New Name: Why do you think names like "New York" and "New Jersey" were so important to the British after they took over the land?
