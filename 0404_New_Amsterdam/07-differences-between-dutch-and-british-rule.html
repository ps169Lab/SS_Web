Differences Between Dutch and British Rule

How Life Changed When New Amsterdam Became New York

Expert Vocabulary

  • Religious Tolerance: Accepting and respecting people who practice different religions.
  • Representative Government: A system where people elect leaders to make laws for them.
  • Autocratic: A way of ruling where one leader has all the power and doesn't ask for advice.
  • Inherit: To receive something (like a tradition or a law) from those who lived before you.

Dutch Rule: The Business Way

Under the Dutch, the colony was run like a giant company. The Director-General acted like a boss who made every single rule without asking the colonists for their opinion. This is called an autocratic government. However, because the Dutch cared most about trade, they practiced religious tolerance. They welcomed almost anyone who was willing to work hard and trade furs, which made the colony very diverse. Their main goal was not to build a new country, but to make a profit for the Dutch West India Company. The Dutch ran the colony like a big business. One leader made all the rules. But the Dutch also let many different people live there. They didn't care what religion you were, as long as you worked hard. Their main goal was to make money by trading.

British Rule: The Government Way

When the British took over, they slowly changed how the colony was managed. While the British governors were still powerful, they eventually allowed the colonists to have a voice. In 1683, they created an Assembly. This was a representative government where men could elect leaders to help make laws. The British also changed the economy. While the Dutch focused on fur trading, the British encouraged more people to own their own small farms. This allowed more families to own land instead of just working for a wealthy Patroon. The British changed the rules. They let the people help make some of the laws. This is called a representative government. The British also wanted people to start their own small farms. More families were able to own their own land under British rule.

Summary: What Changed and What Stayed?

Topic Under the Dutch Under the British
The Leader A "Boss" (Autocratic). Governor and an Elected Assembly.
The Language Dutch. English (for business and law).
Main Work Fur Trading. Farming and Shipping.
Religion Tolerant of many faiths. Stayed Tolerant of many faiths.

A New York "Melting Pot"

Even though the government changed, New York didn't lose its Dutch "flavor" overnight. People continued to speak Dutch in their homes for over a hundred years. We still inherit many things from the Dutch today, like the names of places (Brooklyn and Harlem) and traditions like Santa Claus (Sinterklaas) and learning to ice skate. The biggest change was moving toward a system where the people had a say in their government—an idea that would eventually lead to the American Revolution! Even after the British took over, many things stayed the same. People still spoke Dutch at home. We still use Dutch names for places like Brooklyn. The biggest change was that people got to help make the laws. This helped prepare them to become a new country later on.

Class Discussion Questions

  • The Vote: Why do you think the colonists were happier having an Assembly than having a Director-General who made all the rules?
  • Land Ownership: How does owning your own farm change the way you feel about the place where you live?
  • Dutch Legacy: Can you think of any other words or traditions we still use today that might have come from the Dutch settlers?
