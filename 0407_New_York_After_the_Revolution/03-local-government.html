Governing New York: State and City

Two Levels of Power, One System of Rules

Expert Vocabulary

  • Legislature: The group of people (State Senate/Assembly or City Council) who make the laws.
  • Executive: The leader (Governor or Mayor) who carries out the laws and runs the government day-to-day.
  • Bill: A proposed idea for a new law that is being voted on.
  • Veto: When a leader says "no" to a bill, stopping it from becoming a law.
  • Borough: One of the five major divisions of NYC (Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, Bronx, Staten Island).

One System, Two Levels

Just like the United States government, both New York State and New York City are organized into branches. This ensures **Checks and Balances**, so no single leader has too much power. While the State government in Albany makes rules for everyone in New York, the City government at City Hall makes specific rules for our five boroughs. New York has two governments: one for the whole state and one just for our city. Both use the same "three branch" system. This keeps things fair and makes sure no one person has all the power.

The Leaders: Executive Branch

The Executive branch is led by a single elected official who acts as the "boss" of the government. For the State, this is the Governor. For the City, this is the Mayor. They both propose budgets (plans for money) and oversee agencies like the police and schools. The Executive branch has one top leader. In the state, it is the Governor. In the city, it is the Mayor. They make sure the laws are followed and the city or state runs smoothly.

The Lawmakers: Legislative Branch

The Legislative branch is where new ideas become laws. In Albany, the State Legislature has two houses (the Senate and the Assembly). In NYC, we have a single City Council with 51 members. Each member represents a specific neighborhood, ensuring that every part of the city—from the Bronx to Staten Island—has a voice. The Legislative branch makes the laws. The State has two groups of lawmakers. The City has one group called the City Council. They talk about new rules for things like parks, trash, and safety.

The Courts: Judicial Branch

The Judicial branch interprets the law. Interestingly, New York City does not have its own separate court system; instead, it uses the New York State Court System. The highest court in our state is the Court of Appeals. Judges here decide if laws follow the rules of the State Constitution. The Judicial branch is made of judges. They decide if someone broke the law or if a law is unfair. Even though we live in the city, our judges are part of the New York State court system.

How a Bill Becomes a Law

Whether in the City or the State, the process for making a law follows these main steps:

  1. The Idea: A citizen or lawmaker suggests a change (like healthier school lunches).
  2. The Committee: A small group of experts studies the bill and votes on it.
  3. The Debate: Lawmakers discuss the bill and may make changes.
  4. The Vote: If a majority votes "Yes," the bill moves to the leader's desk.
  5. The Signature: If the Governor or Mayor signs it, it's a law! If they veto it, it goes back for a second vote.

Who Controls What?

Sometimes it's hard to tell which government is in charge of which service. Here is your "Mental Box" for their roles:

State Government 🏙️

  • Schools: Sets the rules for what you learn (curriculum).
  • Roads: Fixes major highways like the NY Thruway.
  • Licenses: Issues driver's licenses and professional permits.

City Government 🍎

  • Schools: Runs the daily school schedule and provides lunch.
  • Trash: Collects garbage and recycling from your street.
  • Subways/Buses: Manages how you get around the five boroughs.
  • Safety: Runs the NYPD and FDNY.

Class Discussion Questions

  • Division of Power: Why do you think the City government handles trash collection while the State government handles driver's licenses?
  • The Veto: If the Governor vetos a bill about school lunches, what can the Legislature do to try and save it?
  • Your Community: If you wanted a new playground in your specific neighborhood, would you write a letter to the Governor in Albany or your City Council member in NYC? Why?
