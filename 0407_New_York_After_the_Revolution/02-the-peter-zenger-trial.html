The Peter Zenger Trial

1735 NYC: The Birth of Freedom of the Press

Expert Vocabulary

  • Libel: Printing false statements that damage someone's reputation.
  • Freedom of the Press: The right of newspapers to publish the truth without government control.
  • Seditious: Encouraging people to rebel against the authority of a state or monarch.
  • Jury: A group of ordinary citizens who decide if someone is guilty or not guilty in a trial.

Who Was Peter Zenger?

John Peter Zenger was a German immigrant who became a printer in New York City. In 1733, he started a newspaper called the New York Weekly Journal. At this time, New York was ruled by a Royal Governor named William Cosby. Cosby was unpopular because he was seen as greedy and unfair, and Zenger's paper was the only one brave enough to print articles criticizing him. Peter Zenger was a printer in New York City a long time ago. He started a newspaper. The leader of New York, Governor Cosby, was a mean leader. Zenger’s newspaper wrote stories about the bad things the Governor was doing.

The Governor's Revenge

Governor Cosby was furious that a newspaper was telling the truth about his mistakes. In 1734, he had Zenger arrested and thrown into a jail cell in NYC. Zenger was charged with "libel." Under the law at that time, it didn't matter if the stories were true—if they made the Governor look bad, it was considered a crime. Zenger spent 10 months in jail waiting for his day in court. The Governor was very angry. He sent soldiers to put Zenger in jail. Zenger stayed in jail for 10 months! The Governor said it was against the law to write bad things about him, even if the stories were true.

The Hero Behind the Scenes: Anna Zenger

While Peter was in jail, the newspaper didn't stop! His wife, Anna Zenger, took over the printing press. She worked through the small window of his jail cell to get his ideas and kept the Weekly Journal arriving on people's doorsteps every week. She proved that the truth couldn't be locked in a cage.

The Trial That Changed Everything

In 1735, the trial began in Manhattan. Zenger’s lawyer, Andrew Hamilton, made a famous argument. He told the jury that "the truth should be a defense." He argued that if a newspaper prints the truth, it should not be a crime, even if it makes a leader look bad. The jury agreed and found Zenger Not Guilty. This victory laid the foundation for the "mental box" of Freedom of the Press in America. In the trial, Zenger’s lawyer told the jury that Zenger only told the truth. He said people have the right to tell the truth about their leaders. The jury agreed! They said Zenger was not guilty. This was a big win for free speech.

Why It Matters Today

Concept Impact
Freedom of Speech Established that truth is the best defense.
Power of the People Showed that a jury of citizens could stand up to a King's Governor.
First Amendment This trial happened 50 years before the Bill of Rights but helped inspire it!

  • The Brave Printer: If you were Anna Zenger, would you have kept printing the newspaper even while your husband was in jail? Why or why not?
  • Local History: This trial happened in what is now Lower Manhattan. How does knowing this happened in our city change how you think about your "First Amendment" rights?
