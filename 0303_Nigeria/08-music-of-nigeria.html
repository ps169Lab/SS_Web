NIGERIAN MUSIC

VOCABULARY

Genre: A type or style of music.

Percussion: Instruments you hit to make sound.

Rhythm: The beat or pattern in music.

Traditional: Music passed down through families for many years.

NATIVE NIGERIAN MUSIC GENRES

Nigeria has many native musical genres that started there thousands of years ago. Jùjú music was invented in Nigeria and uses talking drums and guitars. Apala music comes from Muslim Yoruba people and uses percussion instruments. Fuji music grew from traditional Yoruba music and sounds like Jùjú without guitars. Each genre has its own special rhythms and instruments. Nigeria has many types of music that started there. Jùjú music uses talking drums. Apala music uses percussion. Fuji music comes from old Yoruba music.

INSTRUMENTS FROM NATURE

Early Nigerians were very creative in using their natural surroundings to make musical instruments. They used animal skins stretched over wooden frames to make drums. They carved flutes and whistles from tree branches. They made rattles from dried gourds filled with seeds or beads. They even used hollow logs as drums for long-distance communication. Early Nigerians made instruments from nature. They used animal skins for drums. They carved wood for flutes. They made rattles from gourds. They used hollow logs as drums.

YORUBA MUSIC AND INSTRUMENTS

The Yoruba people created the famous talking drum called "dundun" from wood and animal skin. They can squeeze it to change the pitch and make it sound like human speech. They also make shekere rattles from gourds covered with beads. The Yoruba use metal bells and wooden flutes carved from local trees. Their music has complex rhythms that all play together. The Yoruba people make talking drums from wood and animal skin. They make rattles from gourds with beads. They use metal bells and wooden flutes.

HAUSA MUSIC AND INSTRUMENTS

The Hausa people in northern Nigeria make music with instruments from their desert environment. They create the kakaki, a very long trumpet made from metal that can be over six feet long. They use tambora drums made from wood and animal skins. Hausa children even make rhythms by tapping on inflated pufferfish! Their music often has praise songs for important people. The Hausa people make long trumpets called kakaki from metal. They use drums made from wood and animal skins. Their music has praise songs.

IGBO MUSIC AND INSTRUMENTS

The Igbo people make many different instruments from their forest environment. They create the obo, a 13-stringed instrument like a guitar, from wood and plant fibers. They carve xylophones from wood and make flutes from bamboo. They also make slit drums by hollowing out logs and udu drums from clay pots. The Igbo are known for mixing their traditional music with new styles. The Igbo people make stringed instruments from wood. They carve xylophones and make flutes from bamboo. They make drums from hollow logs and clay pots.

HOW NATURAL RESOURCES SHAPED MUSIC

Nigerian music shows how people use natural resources from their environment to create culture. Forest people use wood for drums and flutes. Desert people use metal for trumpets. River people use clay for pots that become drums. The sounds, rhythms, and instruments all come from the materials that nature provides in each region of Nigeria. Nigerian music uses materials from nature. Forest people use wood. Desert people use metal. River people use clay. All the sounds come from natural materials.

MUSIC IN NIGERIAN CULTURE

Nigerian music is not just for entertainment - it is part of daily life and culture. People use music for work songs while farming, lullabies for babies, and ceremonial music for weddings and festivals. The music tells stories, teaches lessons, and brings communities together. Each song and rhythm has special meaning and connects people to their natural environment and cultural traditions. Nigerian music is part of daily life. People use it for work, for babies, and for celebrations. Music tells stories and brings people together.
