Nigeria's Major Rivers

Intro

Nigeria is the biggest country in West Africa by population and is known for having over 250 different groups of people. The land in Nigeria is very different depending on where you are: in the dry north, you’ll see flat grasslands called savannah, but as you travel south to the edge of the Atlantic Ocean, the land is filled with thick, green rainforests.

Rivers Shape Nigeria

Nigeria gets its name from the Niger River, which is the longest river in the country and stretches for 4,100 kilometers (2,547 miles). The Benue River is the Niger’s main “helper” river, called a tributary. What makes these rivers really special is that they meet almost in the middle of Nigeria, making a giant Y shape and forming the largest part of the land.

Even though both rivers are big and important, the Niger is the longest, and the Benue is its main partner. There are sometimes floods in the low and wet southern areas of Nigeria, but there isn’t a big difference in flooding between these two rivers.

Life by the Rivers

For hundreds of years, the land next to these great rivers has helped people live and grow strong. As far back as the 9th century, important towns grew near the water. For example, the Yoruba people believe their story begins in the ancient city of Ile-Ife, which is close to the Niger River. The rivers made it possible for these early groups to grow crops and get food easily.

Even today, the big mangrove swamps in the Niger Delta, where the two rivers join the sea, are like baby fish “nurseries”—almost 40% of all the fish caught nearby start their lives here. This means many people who live near the rivers have always had enough healthy food to eat.
