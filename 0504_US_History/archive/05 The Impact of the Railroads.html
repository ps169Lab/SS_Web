The Impact of the Railroads

How railroads transformed westward expansion and the United States

The Role of Railroads in Westward Expansion

Railroads played a crucial role in the westward expansion of the United States during the 1800s. Before railroads, traveling across the country was slow, difficult, and dangerous. People traveled by horse, wagon, or boat, and a journey from the East Coast to the West Coast could take months. The development of railroads changed everything by making travel faster, safer, and more comfortable.

Railroads helped connect the eastern and western parts of the United States, allowing people, goods, and information to move quickly across the country. They made it possible for more settlers to move west and establish new communities. The railroads also helped transport natural resources from the West to factories in the East, and manufactured goods from the East to markets in the West.

Major Railroad Companies

Several large railroad companies were responsible for building the rail networks that connected the United States. Some of the most important were:

  • The Union Pacific Railroad: This company built westward from Omaha, Nebraska.
  • The Central Pacific Railroad: This company built eastward from Sacramento, California.
  • The Northern Pacific Railroad: This railroad connected the Great Lakes region with the Pacific Northwest.
  • The Southern Pacific Railroad: This company built lines across the southern United States.
  • The Atchison, Topeka, and Santa Fe Railway: This railroad connected the Midwest with the Southwest.

These companies competed with each other to build more track and serve more areas. They received large grants of land from the federal government, which they could sell to raise money for construction. They also received loans and other financial support from the government.

The Transcontinental Railroad

One of the most important achievements in railroad history was the completion of the first transcontinental railroad in 1869. This railroad connected the existing eastern rail network with the Pacific coast at San Francisco Bay. The Union Pacific Railroad built westward from Omaha, Nebraska, while the Central Pacific Railroad built eastward from Sacramento, California.

The two railroads met at Promontory Summit, Utah, on May 10, 1869. A golden spike was driven into the final tie to connect the two railroads, completing the transcontinental line. This event was celebrated across the country and marked a major milestone in American history. Now, a journey that had once taken months could be completed in about a week.

The construction of the transcontinental railroad was a massive undertaking that required the labor of thousands of workers. Many of these workers were immigrants, including about 15,000 Chinese laborers who worked for the Central Pacific. These workers faced dangerous conditions, harsh weather, and difficult terrain, including the Sierra Nevada mountains.

The Purpose and Impact of the Railroads

The railroads were built for several important purposes. First, they were meant to connect the different parts of the growing United States and make travel and transportation easier. Second, they were designed to help settle the western territories by making it easier for people to move west. Third, they were built to transport goods and resources across the country, supporting economic growth.

The impact of the railroads on the United States was enormous. They helped speed up westward expansion and the settlement of the frontier. Towns and cities grew up along the railroad lines, and many of these communities were actually created by the railroad companies to serve as stops along their routes.

Railroads also transformed the American economy. They made it possible to ship agricultural products from western farms to eastern markets, and to transport manufactured goods from eastern factories to western consumers. They supported the growth of industries like coal mining, steel production, and agriculture. The railroads themselves became some of the largest and most powerful businesses in the country.

Major Figures in Railroad Expansion

Several important businessmen and leaders were involved in the expansion of the railroads. Some of the most notable include:

  • Leland Stanford: One of the "Big Four" who founded the Central Pacific Railroad and later founded Stanford University.
  • Collis P. Huntington: Another of the "Big Four" who helped lead the Central Pacific Railroad.
  • Thomas Durant: A key figure in the Union Pacific Railroad who helped organize the construction of the transcontinental line.
  • James J. Hill: Known as the "Empire Builder," he developed the Great Northern Railway without government subsidies.
  • Cornelius Vanderbilt: A powerful businessman who controlled the New York Central Railroad and other eastern lines.

These men became very wealthy and influential through their involvement with the railroads. They were sometimes called "railroad barons" because of their power and wealth.

Impact on Native Americans

While the railroads brought many benefits to the United States, they had a devastating impact on Native American tribes. The construction of railroads through Native American territories disrupted their traditional ways of life and led to conflicts.

Railroads made it easier for white settlers, miners, and hunters to enter Native American lands. The trains brought more settlers west, increasing pressure on Native American territories. The railroads also contributed to the near-extinction of the American bison (buffalo), which many Plains tribes depended on for food, clothing, and shelter.

The U.S. government often used the military to protect railroad construction and to force Native American tribes onto reservations. The completion of the transcontinental railroad in 1869 marked the beginning of a difficult period for many Native American tribes as their lands were increasingly taken over by white settlers.

The Legacy of the Railroads

The railroads left a lasting legacy on the United States. They played a crucial role in the country's westward expansion and economic development during the 19th century. The rail network they created helped unite the country physically and economically, connecting distant regions and making it possible for people and goods to move quickly across the continent.

Today, while passenger rail service is less dominant than it once was, freight railroads continue to be an important part of the American transportation system. The routes established by the early railroad companies still form the backbone of the nation's rail network, and many of the towns and cities that grew up along the railroad lines continue to thrive.
