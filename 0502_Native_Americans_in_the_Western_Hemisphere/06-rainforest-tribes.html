Rainforest Tribes

Building Great Civilizations in the Tropical Lands

Vocabulary Words

  • Civilization: A complex society with cities, government, and writing
  • Hieroglyphs: Picture writing used to record information
  • Terraces: Flat areas cut into hillsides for farming
  • Olmec: The first great civilization in the Americas
  • Mesoamerica: The region including Mexico and Central America

The Land of Endless Green

In the tropical regions of Central and South America, thick rainforests covered the land with endless green trees, vines, and plants. This region, called Mesoamerica, included what is now Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, Honduras, and El Salvador. Here, it rained frequently, and the climate was warm and humid all year long.

Unlike other regions where Native Americans lived in smaller tribes, the rainforest areas became home to some of the most advanced civilizations in the Western Hemisphere. These were not simple hunter-gatherer societies, but complex civilizations with large cities, powerful governments, and amazing achievements in art, science, and writing.

The Olmec - The Mother Culture

The first great civilization in the Americas was the Olmec, who lived from about 1200 BCE to 400 BCE. The Olmec are sometimes called the "Mother Culture of Mesoamerica" because they influenced all the civilizations that came after them.

The Olmec built the first cities in the Americas, with large stone buildings and plazas where thousands of people could gather. They are famous for carving enormous stone heads, some weighing as much as 40 tons. These giant heads show the faces of Olmec rulers and are among the most amazing artworks ever created in the ancient Americas.

Most importantly, the Olmec developed one of the first writing systems in the Americas. They created hieroglyphs - pictures and symbols that represented words and ideas. This was a huge step forward because it meant they could record their history, laws, and knowledge instead of just passing them down by word of mouth.

Learning to Farm in the Rainforest

Living in the rainforest presented unique challenges for growing food. While there was plenty of rain and the climate was warm, the thick forest blocked sunlight from reaching the ground, and the soil was not always good for farming.

Rainforest civilizations learned to clear areas of forest to create farmland. They developed a system called "slash and burn" agriculture, where they would cut down trees and burn them to create open fields. The ashes from the burned trees made the soil richer for growing crops.

These civilizations became the first in the Americas to develop agriculture independently. They learned to grow corn (maize), which became their most important crop. They also grew beans, squash, tomatoes, and many other plants that are now eaten all over the world.

In hilly areas, they built terraces - flat areas cut into the sides of mountains - to create more farmland. They also built systems to bring water to their crops and to drain swampy areas for farming.

Building Great Cities

Because they could grow enough food to feed many people, rainforest civilizations were able to build large, permanent cities. These cities were much bigger and more complex than the villages built by tribes in other regions.

Rainforest cities had tall pyramid temples, large palaces for rulers, ball courts for playing games, and marketplaces where people traded goods. The buildings were made from stone and were decorated with beautiful carvings and paintings.

These cities were home to thousands of people, including farmers, craftspeople, priests, warriors, and rulers. The cities had complex governments with kings and nobles who made laws and led the people.

Amazing Achievements

Rainforest civilizations made incredible advances in many areas of knowledge. They became expert astronomers, studying the stars and planets to create accurate calendars. They could predict eclipses and knew exactly when to plant and harvest their crops.

They were also skilled mathematicians and engineers. They built roads, bridges, and aqueducts to bring fresh water to their cities. Some of their buildings were so well-constructed that they still stand today, thousands of years later.

These civilizations created beautiful art, including sculptures, paintings, and jewelry made from gold, silver, and precious stones. They also developed complex writing systems that allowed them to record their history, religious beliefs, and scientific knowledge.

Religion and Ceremonies

Rainforest civilizations had complex religious beliefs that were closely connected to nature and the cycles of farming. They worshipped gods of rain, sun, corn, and other forces that were important to their survival.

They built magnificent temples where priests performed ceremonies to honor their gods. These ceremonies often included music, dancing, and offerings of food and precious objects. The temples were usually built on top of tall pyramids that could be seen from far away.

Many of their religious beliefs focused on the idea of a "World Tree" that connected the earth, the sky, and the underworld. They believed that their rulers were connected to the gods and had special powers to communicate with the spirit world.

Trade and Communication

Rainforest civilizations were connected to other regions through extensive trade networks. They traded with tribes in the desert, mountains, and other areas, exchanging goods like jade, obsidian (volcanic glass), cacao beans, and beautiful feathers.

This trade allowed them to get materials they couldn't find in their own region and to spread their ideas and technologies to other parts of the Americas. The influence of rainforest civilizations could be felt across much of North and Central America.

A Foundation for the Future

The early rainforest civilizations like the Olmec laid the foundation for even greater civilizations that would come later, including the Maya and the Aztec. These later civilizations built on the achievements of the earlier ones, creating some of the most advanced societies in the ancient world.

The innovations developed in the rainforest regions - including writing, advanced agriculture, complex government, and monumental architecture - influenced the development of civilizations throughout the Americas.

Builders of the First American Civilizations

The rainforest tribes of Mesoamerica accomplished something truly extraordinary. Starting as simple hunter-gatherers, they developed into the first complex civilizations in the Western Hemisphere. They created writing systems, built magnificent cities, made groundbreaking discoveries in science and mathematics, and established trade networks that connected much of the Americas. Their achievements show us that given the right conditions and enough time, human societies can develop remarkable complexity and sophistication. The rainforest civilizations proved that the Americas were home to some of the world's most advanced ancient cultures.
