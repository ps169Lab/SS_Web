The Iron Horse

How Railroads Shrank the Continent and Changed America

Expert Vocabulary

  • Transcontinental: Something that travels across an entire continent.
  • Locomotive: The powerful steam engine at the front of a train.
  • Immigrant: A person who moves to a new country to live and work.
  • Baron: A person who has great power or wealth in a certain business.

From Wagons to Steam

Before the 1860s, traveling West was a grueling journey that took months in a bumpy wooden wagon. But the invention of the locomotive changed everything. These "Iron Horses" hissed with steam and thundered across the plains, turning a six-month trip into a one-week journey. Suddenly, fresh fruit from California could reach New York, and families could move to the frontier without saying goodbye forever. The country felt smaller and more connected than ever before. Traveling West used to take a long time in a wagon. Then, people built railroads. The locomotive was a fast steam engine that pulled trains. A trip that took months now only took a few days. People and food could travel all over the country very quickly.

The Great Race

Building the first transcontinental railroad was a race between two companies: the Union Pacific and the Central Pacific. Thousands of workers, including many immigrants from China and Ireland, worked in dangerous conditions. They hammered spikes into solid rock and laid tracks through freezing mountain snow. In 1869, the two tracks finally met at Promontory Point, Utah. A final "Golden Spike" was hammered in to celebrate the moment the East and West coasts were finally linked by rail. Two big companies raced to build the tracks. One started in the East and one started in the West. Many immigrant workers from China did the hard work. They worked in the hot sun and cold snow. In 1869, the tracks finally met. They used a golden spike for the last piece. The whole country was now connected!

Wealth and Loss

The railroad made men like Cornelius Vanderbilt and Leland Stanford incredibly rich. These "railroad barons" controlled the flow of goods across the nation. However, this progress had a dark side. The tracks sliced through Native American hunting grounds, and the trains made it easy for hunters to kill millions of buffalo. For the Plains tribes, the railroad wasn't a symbol of progress; it was a sign that their way of life was being destroyed as the government forced them onto smaller and smaller pieces of land. The owners of the railroads became very rich. But the railroads caused problems for Native Americans. The tracks went through their land. The trains also made it easy to hunt the buffalo. Native Americans needed the buffalo to live. The railroad helped some people get rich, but it made Native Americans lose their homes.

Class Discussion Questions

  • The Speed of Change: Before railroads, it took months to send a letter across the country. How do you think the railroad changed the way people felt about being "one country"?
  • The Price of Progress: The railroad helped many people, but it hurt the Native Americans and the buffalo. Is there a way the government could have built the railroad more fairly? How?
