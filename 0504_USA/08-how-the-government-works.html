How the Government Works

The Three Branches and the System of Checks & Balances

Expert Vocabulary

  • Constitution: The "rulebook" or set of basic principles that describes how our government must run.
  • Branch: One of the three main parts of the government, each with different powers.
  • Congress: The group of people (Senate and House) who represent the states and make national laws.
  • Veto: When the President refuses to sign a bill, preventing it from becoming a law.
  • Checks and Balances: A system where each branch has the power to limit the other two, so no one becomes a "King."

The Rulebook: The Constitution

The United States government is organized like a team where every player has a specific role. This structure is defined by the Constitution. To ensure that our nation never returned to a system of absolute power (like the British Monarchy), the Framers divided the government into three distinct branches. Each branch has its own "mental box" of responsibilities. The United States government is like a team. Everyone has a different job. The Constitution is the rulebook that tells everyone what to do. There are three main parts called branches. We have three branches so that no one person can have too much power.

1. The Legislative Branch: The Lawmakers

The Legislative branch is called Congress. It is made of two groups: the Senate (where every state is equal) and the House of Representatives (where states like New York have more voices). Their primary job is to write bills and vote on them to create laws. They also decide how the nation’s tax money is spent. The Legislative branch is called Congress. Their main job is to make laws. They meet in Washington, D.C. to talk about new ideas for rules. They also decide how to spend the country's money.

2. The Executive Branch: The Enforcers

The Executive branch is led by the President. This branch is responsible for carrying out or "enforcing" the laws made by Congress. The President is also the Commander-in-Chief of the military. When Congress passes a bill, the President can either sign it into law or use a veto to send it back. The Executive branch is led by the President. This branch makes sure people follow the laws. The President is also the leader of the army. If the President does not like a new law, they can say "no" by using a veto.

3. The Judicial Branch: The Judges

The Judicial branch consists of the system of federal courts. The highest court is the Supreme Court, which has nine Justices. Their job is to interpret the laws and decide if they follow the Constitution. In New York, we have many federal courthouses where judges decide if people’s rights are being protected. The Judicial branch is made of judges. The most important court is the Supreme Court. These judges look at the rules in the Constitution to decide if laws are fair. They help solve big arguments about the law.

Checks and Balances: Staying Fair

The most important part of this system is Checks and Balances. This ensures that the branches must work together. For example, the President can veto a law, but Congress can override that veto if they have enough votes. The Supreme Court can declare a law unconstitutional, but the President is the one who chooses the judges. It is a constant "tug-of-war" that keeps the government balanced. The branches have to work together. This is called Checks and Balances. If one branch tries to take too much power, the other branches can stop them. This keeps the government fair for everyone.

What Does the Federal Government Control?

These laws apply to everyone in the United States, including us here in New York:

💰 Money Printing the dollars we use every day.
📬 Mail Delivering letters across state lines.
✈️ Travel Making sure airports (like JFK or LGA) are safe.
🌳 Nature Protecting National Parks across the country.
