HOW LAWS ARE MADE IN NEW YORK

VOCABULARY

Bill: A proposed law that is being considered by a legislature.

Committee: A small group of legislators who focus on specific topics or issues.

Veto: When the Governor rejects a bill and prevents it from becoming a law.

Override: When the legislature votes to pass a law despite the Governor's veto.

Democracy: A system of government where power comes from the people, who vote for representatives.

WHAT IS A LAW?

A law is a rule that everyone in a state or country must follow. Laws help keep people safe, protect their rights, and make sure everyone is treated fairly. In New York State, new laws are made through a process that involves many steps and people.

WHAT IS A BILL?

Before something becomes a law, it starts as a "bill." A bill is just an idea for a new law or a change to an existing law. Anyone can have an idea for a law, but only members of the New York State Legislature can introduce a bill.

THE STEPS FOR A BILL TO BECOME A LAW

STEP 1: THE IDEA

The process starts with an idea for a new law. This idea might come from:

  • A legislator (a Senator or Assembly member)
  • The Governor
  • A citizen or group of citizens
  • A community organization
  • A business

For example, students might suggest a law to make school lunches healthier, or community members might want a law to create more parks.

STEP 2: WRITING THE BILL

Once there's an idea, a legislator works with lawyers to write the bill. The bill must be written very carefully to explain exactly what the new law will do.

STEP 3: INTRODUCING THE BILL

The legislator introduces the bill in either the State Senate or the State Assembly. The bill gets a number and is printed so all legislators can read it.

STEP 4: COMMITTEE REVIEW

The bill goes to a committee that specializes in that type of issue. For example, a bill about schools would go to the Education Committee. The committee members:

  • Study the bill
  • May hold hearings where experts and citizens can share their opinions
  • Vote on whether the bill should move forward

If the committee doesn't like the bill, it might "die" here and not move forward.

STEP 5: FLOOR DEBATE AND VOTE

If the committee approves the bill, it goes to the "floor" of either the Senate or Assembly, where:

  • All legislators can debate the bill
  • Changes might be suggested
  • All legislators vote on the bill

STEP 6: THE OTHER HOUSE

If the bill passes in one house (either the Senate or Assembly), it then goes to the other house and repeats steps 4 and 5. Both houses must pass the exact same version of the bill.

STEP 7: THE GOVERNOR'S DECISION

If both the Senate and Assembly pass the bill, it goes to the Governor, who has three choices:

  • Sign the bill, which makes it a law
  • Veto (reject) the bill, which stops it from becoming a law
  • Do nothing for 10 days, after which the bill automatically becomes a law (if the Legislature is in session)

STEP 8: OVERRIDING A VETO (SOMETIMES)

If the Governor vetoes the bill, the Legislature can try to "override" the veto by voting again. They need a two-thirds majority in both houses to override a veto. This is difficult to achieve and doesn't happen often.

WHY THIS PROCESS IS IMPORTANT

This process might seem long and complicated, but it's designed to:

  • Make sure laws are carefully considered
  • Allow many people to have input
  • Prevent bad laws from being passed too quickly
  • Create a system of checks and balances

HOW CITIZENS CAN HELP MAKE LAWS

Even though you're not old enough to vote yet, you can still help make laws in New York:

  • Write letters to your legislators about issues you care about
  • Attend public hearings on bills
  • Join community groups that work on important issues
  • Learn about how government works so you'll be ready to vote when you're 18

Remember, in a democracy, the government works for the people. Your voice matters in making laws that affect your life and community!
