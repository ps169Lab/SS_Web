How People First Came to the Americas

Scientists believe the very first people to come to the Americas walked or traveled by boat across this land bridge. They were Paleo-Indians, who were hunter-gatherers. They might have moved through an "ice-free corridor" that opened up between the big glaciers in what is now Canada, or they might have traveled along the Pacific coast using primitive boats. Scientists think the first people to come to America walked across this land bridge. These people were called Paleo-Indians. They hunted animals and gathered plants for food.

Life on the Land Bridge

Beringia wasn't covered in ice like other places during the Ice Age. Instead, it was a cold, dry place with lots of grass and tundra plants, like a big grassy plain. What Happened to the Bridge

Eventually, the Ice Age started to end, and the giant glaciers began to melt. All that melting ice caused the sea levels to rise again, and the Bering Land Bridge slowly got covered by water, disappearing underwater between 13,000 and 11,000 years ago. When the Ice Age ended, the glaciers melted. The ocean got higher and covered the land bridge with water. This happened about 13,000 years ago.


Once the land bridge was gone, the people who had crossed over were now permanently in the Americas, where they would continue to spread out and develop many different cultures and ways of life. Scientists know about this amazing journey by studying clues like fossils - finding ancient animal bones and human remains in different places. They also look at genetics by studying the DNA of ancient people and comparing it to people today, which shows links between Asian and Native American populations.
