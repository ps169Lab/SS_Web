The Bering Land Bridge

How People First Came to the Americas

Vocabulary Words

  • Beringia - the land bridge that connected Asia and North America
  • Glaciers - huge, thick blankets of ice
  • Paleo-Indians - the first people to live in the Americas
  • Hunter-gatherers - people who got food by hunting animals and gathering plants

The Ice Age Land Bridge

A long time ago, much of the Earth was covered by huge ice blankets called glaciers. Because so much water was frozen in ice, the sea levels dropped, and parts of the ocean floor became dry land. One important dry land was called the Bering Land Bridge or Beringia. It was a wide strip of land up to 1,000 kilometers across, connecting Asia (where Russia is today) to North America (where Canada and the United States are now).


How People Came to the Americas

Scientists believe the first people to come to the Americas traveled across this land bridge. They were called Paleo-Indians, who hunted animals and gathered plants for food. They may have gone through an ice-free path in Canada or traveled along the Pacific coast by simple boats. These early people followed big animals like mammoths, bison, and caribou, searching for food and new places to live.


Life on the Land Bridge

Unlike other icy places, Beringia was cold but not covered in ice. It had lots of grass and tundra plants, like a big grassy plain. This was a good home for big animals like horses, mammoths, camels, bison, and musk-oxen. Some early people lived there for hundreds or thousands of years, adapting to the land and learning how to survive.


What Happened to the Bridge

When the Ice Age ended, glaciers melted, and sea levels rose. The Bering Land Bridge slowly went underwater between 13,000 and 11,000 years ago. Once it disappeared, people who crossed it were now stuck in the Americas, where they spread out and started many different cultures and ways of life.


How Scientists Know This Happened

Scientists find clues like fossils of ancient animals and humans that tell the story of this journey. They study DNA from ancient and modern people, showing connections between Asian and Native American groups. They also discover old stone tools left by these early travelers.
