Pangea and Continental Drift

How Earth's Giant Land Puzzle Came Apart

Vocabulary Words

  • Pangaea - the supercontinent that existed 250 million years ago
  • Continental Drift - the slow movement of continents over millions of years
  • Tectonic Plates - huge, slowly moving rock slabs that make up Earth's surface
  • Supercontinent - when all continents are joined together as one giant landmass

What is Pangaea?

Imagine Earth as a big, ever-changing puzzle! A very long time ago, all the continents were joined together in one giant land called Pangaea. Pangaea looked like a huge letter C, stretching from the North Pole to the South Pole, about 250 million years ago. But it wouldn’t stay joined forever!

What is Continental Drift?

Over millions of years, the big pieces of land slowly moved apart. This slow movement is called continental drift. It happens because Earth’s outer layer is made of huge rock slabs called tectonic plates that float on a thick, gooey layer inside the Earth—like rafts on water.

Splitting of Pangaea

Around 200 million years ago, Pangaea began to break into two big parts called Laurasia in the north and Gondwana in the south. South America was once part of Gondwana and broke off about 135 million years ago to become its own continent. North America was part of Laurasia and separated from Eurasia (Europe and Asia today) later on.

The Formation of Central America

For a long time, North and South America were separate, with a big ocean between them. About 15 million years ago, underwater movements caused volcanoes to erupt, creating islands near where Central America is now. Over time, dirt and rocks filled in the spaces between, making a land bridge called the Isthmus of Panama about 3 million years ago.

The Great American Connection

This land bridge connected North and South America. Because of this, many animals and plants moved between the two continents in what is called the Great American Interchange.

How Scientists Know This Happened

Scientists found clues by studying fossils—the remains of ancient plants and animals. For example, fossils of a small reptile called Mesosaurus are only found in South America and southern Africa. Since this creature couldn’t swim across oceans, it proves those lands were joined long ago.

So, the continents of the Western Hemisphere started as one huge supercontinent, drifted apart slowly, and then reconnected much later because of Earth’s slow-moving land puzzle pieces.
