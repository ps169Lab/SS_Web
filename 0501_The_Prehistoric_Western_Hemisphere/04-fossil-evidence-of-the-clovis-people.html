What the Evidence Shows

The spear points were found directly with mammoth bones, showing that the Clovis people were skilled big-game hunters. Projectile points show impact scars from thrusting and throwing against hard bone, and stone tools display damage from butchering animals. The spear points were found with mammoth bones, showing that the Clovis people were good at hunting big animals. The tools show marks from hitting bones and cutting up animals.


From the artifacts, scientists learned that the Clovis people were nomadic, moving in search of food and living in crude shelters, tents, or shallow caves. They flourished on lush grasslands with megafauna such as mammoths, giant bison, dire wolves, camels, giant ground sloths, huge turtles, and saber-toothed tigers. Pre-Clovis Evidence

Many archaeological sites are now widely accepted as predating the Clovis culture. Key examples include Monte Verde II in Chile, dated to around 14,500 years ago, making it one of the oldest confirmed sites of human habitation in the Americas. Scientists have found places where people lived before the Clovis people. One important place is Monte Verde in Chile, where people lived about 14,500 years ago.



Other important sites include Paisley Caves in Oregon, where fossilized human remains with human DNA have been dated back 14,300 years, and Meadowcroft Rockshelter in Pennsylvania, which has evidence of continuous human habitation dating as far back as 19,000 or 16,000 years ago. Other important places include caves in Oregon and Pennsylvania where people lived thousands of years ago, even before the Clovis people.
