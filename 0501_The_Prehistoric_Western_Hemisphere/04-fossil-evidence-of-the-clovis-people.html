Fossil Evidence of the Clovis People

Learning About Early Americans Through Archaeological Evidence

Vocabulary Words

  • Archaeological - relating to the study of ancient people through their artifacts
  • Clovis points - special spear points made by early Americans
  • Artifacts - objects made by humans long ago
  • Pre-Clovis - people who lived in the Americas before the Clovis culture

Who Were the Clovis People?

Scientists learn about the earliest people in the Americas by studying archaeological sites and fossils. One important group is the Clovis people, known for their unique stone tools found at sites across North America. But the Clovis people were not the very first to live here; people lived in the Americas even earlier, between 20,000 and 16,000 years ago.


The Clovis Site

The Clovis culture was first found near Clovis, New Mexico in the 1930s. A young archaeologist named Ridgely Whiteman discovered special spear points called Clovis points in 1929. These points had sharp edges and grooves on the sides and were used for hunting large animals like mammoths. Scientists also found stone tools, fire pits, and signs that people lived there for a long time.


What the Evidence Shows

The Clovis people were good hunters who chased big animals. The spear points show marks from hitting and throwing at bones, and other tools had damage from cutting meat. They lived by moving around for food, living in simple shelters like tents or caves. They hunted mammoths, giant bison, saber-toothed tigers, and many other large animals.


Earlier Than Clovis

Scientists found many sites that are older than Clovis. For example, Monte Verde II in Chile dates back about 14,500 years, making it one of the earliest known human homes in the Americas. In Oregon, Paisley Caves have fossils with human DNA from about 14,300 years ago. In Pennsylvania, Meadowcroft Rockshelter shows evidence of people living there as early as 16,000 to 19,000 years ago.


How Scientists Study Early People

Stone tools like spear points and scrapers help archaeologists learn about early life. DNA shows a link between Native Americans and people from Siberia, proving the first Americans came from Asia. Some tool materials came from far away, meaning early people traveled a lot or traded with others.


All this proof helps scientists understand that people have lived in the Americas much longer than they once thought. These early humans learned how to live and adapt in many different ways as they spread across the continents.
