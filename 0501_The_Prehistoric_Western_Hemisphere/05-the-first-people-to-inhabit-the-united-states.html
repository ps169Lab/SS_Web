The First People to Inhabit the United States

How Early Americans Lived and Adapted to Their Environment

Vocabulary Words

  • Paleo-Indians - the earliest known people to live in the Americas
  • Nomadic - moving from place to place instead of staying in one location
  • Megafauna - very large animals that lived during the Ice Age
  • Lithic - relating to stone tools

Who Were the First Americans?

The first people to live in the Americas are called Paleo-Indians. They traveled from Asia a very long time ago—between 20,000 and 16,000 years in the past. When they arrived, they had to learn how to survive in a brand new place.

Where Did They Live?

Paleo-Indians did not stay in one spot for long. They moved from place to place in small family groups, called bands, always looking for food. Sometimes they made simple shelters from branches, animal skins, or even lived in caves. At an old camp in Chile, researchers found long wooden houses that were held down with stakes.

What Did They Eat?

These first Americans ate many different foods. They hunted big animals like mammoths, bison, mastodons, and reindeer, but they also caught smaller animals and fish. Gathering wild plants, fruits, nuts, and even seaweed helped them stay healthy. In South America, they found lots of tasty fruits and nuts growing in the forests.

How Did They Get Food?

Finding food was hard work. They watched where big animal herds went and followed them. Stone tools were very important—a sharp rock point on a stick could become a spear for hunting. When the weather changed and big animals became rare, Paleo-Indians started eating more plants and smaller animals to survive.

Changing the Land

Early people changed their world by hunting so many big animals and by using fire to clear grass and bushes. Burning the land helped them hunt by making it easier to see and catch animals.

Making Tools and Trading

People made stone tools from rocks found far away from their homes. This means they either traveled long distances or traded with other bands. After the climate became less wild (about 10,000 years ago), these bands started settling down in one place for longer and began to invent new ways to live.

New Tribes and Nations

As time went on, different groups learned to live almost anywhere—from cold Arctic places to warm tropical forests. They made new cultures and ways of life, and these became the many different Native American tribes and nations we know today.

These first Americans were amazing—they learned, adapted, and built communities in a world that was brand new to them.
