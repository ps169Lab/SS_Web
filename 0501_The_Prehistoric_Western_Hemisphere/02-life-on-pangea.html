What Was Life Like on Pangaea?

Life on Pangaea was amazing and very different from today! What kind of life existed on Pangaea? What did the landscape look like? Around this huge supercontinent was a giant ocean called Panthalassa, and the land itself was home to incredible plants and animals. Life on Pangaea was very different from today! This huge land was surrounded by a giant ocean called Panthalassa. Many amazing plants and animals lived there.


Life in the Giant Ocean

In the ocean called Panthalassa, there were cool creatures like ammonites with their swirly shells, squishy mollusks, and spiky sea urchins. You could also find giant sea reptiles, like ichthyosaurs (who looked a bit like dolphins!) and plesiosaurs with their long necks and flippers, swimming in Panthalassa. The ocean had many cool sea animals. There were ammonites with curly shells, soft mollusks, and spiky sea urchins. Giant sea reptiles also lived there, like ichthyosaurs that looked like dolphins!


Animals on Land

On the land of Pangaea, especially near water bodies, you would see animals like frogs, salamanders, crocodiles, turtles, and snakes. Look up in the sky, and you might spot pterosaurs, which were flying reptiles that soared through the ancient skies. On land, there were frogs, salamanders, crocodiles, turtles, and snakes. In the sky, you could see pterosaurs - flying reptiles that flew through the air.


The Triassic Period, which was when Pangaea began to split apart, was a very exciting time for new life. Many older animals and plants had disappeared, but the ones that survived started to spread out and change. This is when the first tiny mammals and the very first dinosaurs started to appear and grow. Dinosaurs would eventually become the dominant land animals during this time. During the Triassic Period, Pangaea started to break apart. This was an exciting time! Many old animals had died, but new ones appeared. The first small mammals and the first dinosaurs started to live on Earth.

Plant Life on Pangaea

The ground was covered with many different plants, including soft mosses, ferns, and tall trees like conifers and ginkgos. There were also lots of smaller creatures too, like spiders, scorpions, millipedes, centipedes, and even grasshoppers living among these plants. The ground had many plants like soft mosses, ferns, and tall trees. Small creatures like spiders, scorpions, and grasshoppers lived among the plants.


Climate and Weather

The climate on Pangaea was quite varied due to its enormous size. The middle parts of the supercontinent, far from the ocean, were often very dry and cool, almost like a huge desert, with big differences between hot and cold temperatures. Pangaea had different weather in different places. The middle parts were very dry and cool, like a big desert. Some places were hot and some were cold.


But some areas, especially near the equator, experienced wet, swampy conditions because of giant monsoon rains. These monsoons brought tropical, moisture-filled air from the surrounding seas. Because all the land was together, it also meant that animals and plants that liked dry weather could spread easily across the land. Some areas had lots of rain and were very wet and swampy. Since all the land was connected, animals and plants could move easily from place to place.

The Landscape

Imagine a huge, C-shaped landmass stretching almost from the top of the Earth to the bottom. Running right through the middle of Pangaea were enormous mountains called the Central Pangaean Mountains. Think of them like the tallest mountains we have today, but even longer! These big mountains actually made a huge difference to the weather across the supercontinent. Pangaea was shaped like a huge letter "C". It had very big mountains running through the middle called the Central Pangaean Mountains. These mountains changed the weather across the land.


Because the mountains blocked a lot of the rain, the middle parts of Pangaea were very, very dry and cool, like a huge desert. Closer to the edges, where the land met the giant ocean, the climate was much wetter and some areas were like big, lush swamps. The mountains stopped rain from reaching the middle of Pangaea, so it was very dry like a desert. Near the ocean, it was much wetter with swamps.

It was a truly unique landscape, very different from the world we know today, before the big puzzle pieces started drifting apart and the first humans in the Americas would eventually adapt to their environment and develop unique cultures. Pangaea was very different from our world today. Later, the land would break apart into the continents we know now.
