The Roots of Slavery

Labor, Disease, and the Human Spirit

Key Words

  • Enslavement: Being owned by another person and forced to work for free without freedom.
  • Plantation: A huge farm that grows one main crop like sugar or tobacco to sell for profit.
  • Transatlantic Slave Trade: The forced movement of enslaved people from Africa to the Americas across the Atlantic Ocean.

The Search for Labor

As the European "Major Players" built their empires, they became obsessed with "Gold"â€”not just the metal, but the money made from growing crops like sugar to sell. To run the silver mines in Mexico and the huge farms in Brazil and the Caribbean, they needed thousands of workers. At first, the Spanish and Portuguese forced Native Americans into labor. However, as we saw in the Columbian Exchange, germs like smallpox were devastating. With millions of Native Americans dying from disease, European leaders looked to Africa to find a new workforce that they could force into enslavement. European kings wanted to get rich from gold and big farms called plantations. They needed many workers. At first, they forced Native Americans to do the work. But many Native Americans died from germs like smallpox. Because so many workers died, Europeans began to force people from Africa to come to the Americas to work instead.

Who Was Involved?

Not every European power participated in the slave trade in the same way. Portugal and Spain were the first to start the trade to provide labor for their sugar and gold operations. Later, England became the largest trader of enslaved people to power their tobacco and rice farms in the Southern colonies. The Dutch were also heavily involved, using their ships to transport people as part of their global business. France used enslaved labor primarily on their sugar islands. In contrast, while countries like Sweden or Russia explored the Americas, they were far less involved in the Transatlantic Slave Trade because they did not have large plantation-based colonies in the South. Different countries played different roles. Portugal and Spain started the trade. England became the biggest trader to help their big farms in the South. The Dutch used their fast ships to move people. France also used enslaved labor. Some other countries that did not have big farms were not as involved in the slave trade.

Resistance and Strength

It is important to remember that enslaved people were not just numbers in a ledger; they were individuals with families and cultures. They practiced many ways of resisting and fighting back. Some resisted by working slowly or breaking tools, while others escaped to form free communities in the mountains or forests. They kept their African heritage alive through music, stories, and secret religious ceremonies. This strength allowed them to stay connected to who they were in a system that tried to take that away. Enslaved people were very brave. They did not want to be forced to work, so they found ways to fight back. Some worked slowly or broke tools. Some ran away to live in the woods. They sang songs and told stories from Africa to remember who they were. This helped them stay strong.
