Slavery Comes to New York

Long before New York became the state we know today, slavery was part of daily life. The Dutch brought the first enslaved Africans to New Amsterdam (now New York City) in 1626. When the English took control in the 1660s, they continued bringing enslaved people to work in the colony. A long time ago, New York was very different. People from Holland brought African people to work as slaves in 1626. Later, people from England took over and kept bringing more slaves to work.

By 1703, almost half of all families in New York City owned enslaved people. This made New York City the second-largest slave-owning city in all the American colonies, after Charleston, South Carolina. By 1703, many families in New York City had slaves. New York City had more slaves than almost any other city in America.

Daily Life for Enslaved People

Enslaved people in New York worked many different jobs. They worked as farmers growing crops, builders constructing houses, and servants in homes. Some worked in skilled trades like carpentry or blacksmithing. Others worked loading and unloading ships at the busy New York harbor. Slaves in New York did many kinds of work. They grew food, built houses, and cleaned homes. Some learned special jobs like making things from wood or metal. Others carried heavy things on and off ships.

Under Dutch rule, enslaved families were usually kept together, and they could join churches and get married. But when the English took control, life became much harder. New laws said enslaved people could not travel, trade, or buy things without permission from their owners. When the Dutch people were in charge, slave families could stay together and go to church. But when English people took over, life got much harder. Slaves couldn't go places or buy things without asking their owners first.

Where Enslaved People Lived

Enslaved people lived in small, cramped spaces in their owners' homes. They might sleep in back rooms, cellars, attics, or tiny rooms called garrets. These spaces were often cold in winter and hot in summer. Slaves lived in very small spaces in their owners' houses. They slept in basements, attics, or tiny back rooms. These places were cold in winter and hot in summer.

Fighting for Freedom

Even though life was very difficult, enslaved people never stopped hoping for freedom. Some tried to escape to find better lives. Others found small ways to resist the unfair treatment they received every day. Even though life was very hard, slaves never stopped wanting to be free. Some tried to run away. Others found small ways to fight back against the bad treatment.

In 1712, a group of enslaved New Yorkers fought back against their treatment. This was called the New York Slave Revolt of 1712. A group of about 20 slaves set a building on fire and while the slave owners were distracted trying to put it out, they tried to run away. Sadly, they were all caught and brought back to the slave owners. While this rebellion was not successful, it showed that enslaved people would never stop wanting their freedom. The Path to Freedom

After the American Revolution, New York slowly began to end slavery. In 1799, the state passed a law saying that children born to enslaved mothers would eventually be free, but they had to work for many years first. Finally, in 1827, slavery officially ended in New York State. After America became free from England, New York slowly started to end slavery. In 1799, they made a law that said children of slaves would be free when they grew up, but they still had to work for many years first. Finally, in 1827, slavery ended in New York.
